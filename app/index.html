The outcome surprised many observers, as polls had consistently shown Vice President Harris leading in the months before election day, with her message of generational change resonating strongly with voters.

But should we really be surprised?

The dramatic shift in Democratic leadership mid-campaign created an unprecedented scenario that traditional polls struggled to capture accurately.

We're going to examine prediction markets alongside traditional polling data to see how they reacted to key events throughout the campaign.

This analysis will help us understand whether prediction markets may be better indicators of election outcomes compared to traditional polls, especially in scenarios of significant political upheaval.

What are Prediction Markets?

Before we dive in, let's understand what prediction markets are and why they might be better at forecasting election outcomes than traditional polls.

Traditional Polls vs. Prediction Markets

Traditional Polls

  • Ask people who they plan to vote for
  • Snapshot of current opinions
  • No real consequences for being wrong
  • Can be biased by who responds

Prediction Markets

  • People bet real money on outcomes
  • Prices reflect collective wisdom
  • Financial incentive to be accurate
  • Self-correcting through arbitrage

Think of prediction markets like a stock market, but instead of trading company shares, people buy and sell "shares" in election outcomes. If you think Trump has a 70% chance of winning but his contracts are trading at 60¢ (implying a 60% chance), you might buy them expecting to profit.

Our hypothesis is simple: when people put their money where their mouth is, they're more likely to:

  • Consider all available information, not just their preferences
  • Account for factors that polls might miss
  • Update their positions quickly when new information emerges

Let's test this hypothesis by examining how both polls and prediction markets reacted to key events in the 2024 election.

Arbitrage Opportunities

Arbitrage is a simple but powerful concept: it's the ability to profit from price differences between markets.

Imagine you could buy a Trump contract for 50¢ on one market and simultaneously sell it for 52¢ on another. That 2¢ difference is pure profit, with no risk!

Below, we track these opportunities between Kalshi and Polymarket throughout the campaign. The slider lets you explore different moments in time:

  • Total Cost: How much you need to spend to execute the arbitrage
  • Potential Profit: The guaranteed profit per $1 invested
  • Max Volume: The maximum number of contracts available
  • Max Profit: The total profit possible at this moment
